Split Screen for Vellum

Reading two documents at once is the whole point of a research reader — a paper next to its references, a spec next to the code it describes, your notes next to the source. This is the interaction & layout design for how splits get created, focused, resized, and closed.

1The core decision: panes are tab groups

Vellum today has one active tab and one viewer. The cleanest way to add splits — and the one every power user already knows from VS Code, Zed and modern browsers — is the editor-group model: the window holds a tree of panes, and each pane is its own independent tab strip with its own active document.

✔ Why per-pane tab strips

  • No ambiguity about “which side does this tab open in” — a tab belongs to a pane.
  • Drag a tab from one pane to another to move it; drag to an edge to make a new split.
  • Maps 1:1 onto the current PdfTab model — a pane is just a [PdfTab] + an activeTabId.
  • Each pane keeps its own page, zoom and scroll — exactly today’s per-tab viewport state.

✘ Rejected: one shared tab strip

  • A single strip on top with panes below forces a “send to left / send to right” mode — extra clicks, unclear target.
  • Breaks the direct-manipulation feel; you’d never drag a tab to split.
  • Doesn’t match any app users already know for side-by-side reading.
One window, not many. Splits live inside the existing window. macOS already gives us multi-window (⌘N) and Stage Manager for people who want detached windows; splits are for glanceable comparison where both documents stay in view and share one toolbar.

3Three ways to create a split

PRIMARY · direct

Drag a tab to an edge

Grab any tab and drag it over the document area. Five drop zones light up — the four edges create a split in that direction and carry the tab into the new pane; the center just moves the tab into that group. This is the discoverable, no-menu path.

KEYBOARD · fast

View ▸ Split & shortcuts

⌘⌥→ splits the focused pane to the right, ⌘⌥↓ downward — cloning the focused document into the new pane so you can scroll the two copies independently. Lives in the View menu next to Zoom.

CONTEXT · precise

Right-click a tab

Tab context menu gains Split Right, Split Down, and Open in Other Pane. Good when you know exactly which document you want beside the current one.

Split of what? A menu/shortcut split clones the focused document into the new pane (two views of the same PDF — great for cross-referencing pages). A drag split moves the dragged tab out into the new pane. Two intents, two gestures.

4Focus is the linchpin

With more than one pane visible, exactly one pane is focused (subtle indigo ring). Focus is what the single, shared chrome points at:

  • The window toolbar — page counter, zoom, page-turn arrows, ⌘F Find, print — all act on the focused pane’s document. One toolbar, retargeted; we don’t duplicate it per pane.
  • The inspector (Annotations / AI) — reflects the focused pane’s document, and highlighting text there adds annotations to that document. Switching focus swaps its contents.
  • Menu commands & most shortcuts — Save, Print, Go to Page, note mode: focused pane.

Clicking anywhere in a pane focuses it. ⌘⌥←/→/↑/↓ also moves focus between panes without the mouse. The focused pane persists per window across tab switches.

5Resizing & collapsing

Resize

Every split has a draggable divider (min pane size ~280 px so a page stays readable). Double-click a divider to snap back to 50/50. Sizes are proportional, so the layout survives window resizing.

Close

Closing a pane’s last tab collapses the pane and its sibling reclaims the space — no empty voids. Dragging the last tab out of a pane does the same. There’s also View ▸ Merge Panes (⌘⌥J) to flatten everything back to one.

6Keyboard map

⌘⌥→ / ⌘⌥↓Split focused pane right / down (clones current doc)
⌘⌥←/→/↑/↓Move focus to the pane in that direction
⌘⌥JMerge all panes back into one
⌘WClose active tab → collapses the pane when it was the last tab
⌘TNew tab in the focused pane
⌘⇧[ / ⌘⇧]Previous / next tab within the focused pane

7Resolved decisions

  • Nesting: unlimited. The full pane tree — 2×2, 3-up, and deeper — is allowed. No cap.
  • Sizing: free-form. Any ratio (75/25, 60/40, …) by dragging dividers; not snapped to 50/50.
  • Persistence: full. The entire layout — tree shape, per-split ratios, each pane’s tabs, active tab, and focus — is saved and restored on relaunch.
  • Mixed content: yes. Any pane holds any mix of PDF and web tabs, and both Annotations and AI work for both document kinds. The one shared inspector follows the focused pane’s document.
